// about

What is DevOps?

A cultural and professional movement that stresses communication, collaboration, and integration between software developers and IT operations.

// definition

Breaking Down
the Dev + Ops Divide

DevOps is not a tool, a job title, or a methodology — it is a mindset shift. It combines cultural philosophies, practices, and tools that increase an organisation's ability to deliver applications and services at high velocity.

At its core, DevOps removes the wall between the teams who write code and the teams who run it — fostering shared responsibility across the entire software delivery lifecycle.

The result: faster innovation, fewer outages, and more reliable systems that serve customers better every day.

● devops_explained.sh
# The classic wall (before DevOps)
Dev: "Works on my machine 🤷"

# With DevOps
Team: "We own this together ✓"

// pillars

The Three Pillars of DevOps

Culture, processes, and tools working in harmony to transform how software is built.

🧠

Culture & People

DevOps succeeds or fails based on people. It requires psychological safety, blameless post-mortems, shared ownership, and a relentless focus on learning from failure rather than hiding it.

  • Shared accountability across Dev and Ops
  • Blameless retrospectives and post-mortems
  • Transparency and open communication
  • Continuous learning culture
⚙️

Process & Practice

Structured workflows like CI/CD pipelines, agile sprints, and infrastructure automation replace manual handoffs with repeatable, auditable processes that scale with the team.

  • Continuous integration and delivery
  • Automated testing at every stage
  • Infrastructure as Code practices
  • Agile and lean methodologies
🛠️

Tools & Technology

The right toolchain amplifies culture and process — but tools alone cannot make an organisation "do DevOps." They are the enablers of speed, reliability, and visibility.

  • Source control and branching strategies
  • Container and orchestration platforms
  • Monitoring and observability stacks
  • Cloud-native infrastructure tooling
// history

Evolution of DevOps

From Agile manifesto to cloud-native transformation — a decade of disruption.

2001

The Agile Manifesto

Twelve software practitioners publish the Agile Manifesto in Snowbird, Utah — prioritising individuals, collaboration, working software, and responding to change. DevOps would later build on these foundations.

2007 – 2008

The Conflict Surfaces

Patrick Debois, frustrated by the disconnect between development and operations while working on a data centre migration project in Belgium, begins speaking publicly about the growing tension between the two worlds.

2009

The Term "DevOps" is Born

Patrick Debois organises the first "DevOpsDays" conference in Ghent, Belgium. He coins the term DevOps by combining "Development" and "Operations." The Twitter hashtag #devops is born, spreading the ideas globally.

2010 – 2012

Velocity & The Phoenix Project

John Allspaw and Paul Hammond deliver their influential O'Reilly Velocity talk "10+ Deploys Per Day: Dev and Ops Cooperation at Flickr." In 2013, Gene Kim publishes The Phoenix Project, a business novel that popularises DevOps beyond technical circles.

2013 – 2015

Containers & Microservices

Docker (2013) and the rise of microservices architecture transform how teams package and deliver software. Kubernetes emerges from Google (2014), providing the orchestration layer that makes container deployments production-ready at scale.

2016 – 2019

DORA Metrics & SRE

The DevOps Research and Assessment (DORA) team, led by Dr. Nicole Forsgren, publishes the "State of DevOps" reports with four key metrics. Google's Site Reliability Engineering (SRE) book formalises practices for running reliable production systems.

2020 – Present

Platform Engineering & DevSecOps

Security shifts left with DevSecOps integration. Platform engineering teams emerge to build internal developer platforms (IDPs) that abstract complexity. GitOps, FinOps, and AIOps extend the DevOps model into new domains, with AI-assisted code review and incident response becoming mainstream.
